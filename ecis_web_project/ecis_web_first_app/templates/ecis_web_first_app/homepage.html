Bacteria employ toxin-translocating secretion systems to exclude bacterial competitors and to infect host cells. Characterization of these systems and the toxins they secrete is important for understanding microbial interactions and virulence in different ecosystems. The extracellular Contractile Injection System (eCIS) is a cell-free toxin delivery particle that evolved from a bacteriophage tail. It is believed to be released from producing cells. eCIS particles in the environment then undergo binding to target cells, contraction, and injection of effectors, which sometimes are toxic. These nanomachines are understudied, and the Levy Lab has taken a broad bioinformatic and experimental approach to study eCIS. By mining genomes in the IMG database, we indentified 1,425 eCIS loci in 1,249 prokaryotic genomes (both Bacteria and Archaea). The publication can be accessed at (....).

eCIStem provides visualization of all eCIS operons. The database includes gene annotation, an eCIS operon search option based on taxonomy and gene clusters, and links to additional data from the IMG database (ref). eCIStem also contains ecological and life-style metadata of the eCIS encoding microbe. This valuable information can help researchers study eCIS with regard to its specific ecological functions and to deduce the eCIS target organism. Figure 7 from our publication (ref) presents some of the information held in eCIStem. eCIStem is an expanded version of the recently developed dbeCIS which includes 631 operons (ref). eCIStem provides protein domain data for all eCIS genes, as well as full database download option which will facilitate future bioinformatic analysis of our dataset by other members of the research community. The information held in eCIStem is freely available to users from academic non-profit institutions.
